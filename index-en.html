Cross-Reference: Vietnam War Protest Song Artists & Specific Anti-War Protests

1. John Lennon — "Give Peace a Chance" (1969)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Involvement
Amsterdam Bed-In for Peace Mar 25–31, 1969 Amsterdam Hilton, Netherlands Co-organized and led with Yoko Ono; invited international press to their hotel room as a week-long peace demonstration
Montreal Bed-In for Peace — see also Give Peace a Chance May 26 – Jun 2, 1969 Queen Elizabeth Hotel, Montreal, Canada Co-organized with Yoko Ono; "Give Peace a Chance" was recorded live in the hotel room during this event, with guests including Timothy Leary, Tommy Smothers, Petula Clark, and others
Vietnam Moratorium March on Washington — see also New Mobilization Committee Nov 15, 1969 Washington, D.C. Pete Seeger led ~500,000 marchers in singing "Give Peace a Chance"; the song became the de facto anthem of the day — Lennon was not physically present but the song defined the event
John Sinclair Freedom Rally — see also John Sinclair Dec 10, 1971 Crisler Arena, Ann Arbor, Michigan Lennon and Ono performed; rally demanded release of activist John Sinclair; Stevie Wonder and others also appeared
National Peace Rally, Duffy Square, NYC — referenced in John Lennon biography and Lennon deportation case Apr 22, 1972 Duffy Square, New York City Lennon and Ono spoke and performed at a rally against the renewed bombing of North Vietnam
One to One Concert Aug 30, 1972 Madison Square Garden, NYC Though primarily a benefit for Willowbrook, Lennon used the platform for anti-war statements
International note: The Bed-Ins were among the most visible international anti-Vietnam War protests by any single artist. The Nixon administration considered Lennon enough of a threat to attempt deportation — see John Lennon's deportation case.

2. Joan Baez — "Saigon Bride" (1967)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Involvement
March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom Aug 28, 1963 Washington, D.C. Performed "We Shall Overcome" and "Oh Freedom"; primarily civil rights but established her as a protest icon
Free Speech Movement rallies 1964 UC Berkeley, Sproul Plaza, California Performed at sit-ins and rallies; the FSM became a precursor to the campus anti-war movement
Institute for the Study of Nonviolence — referenced in Joan Baez biography Founded 1965 Carmel Valley, California Baez co-founded this center for training in nonviolent resistance, explicitly tied to anti-war and anti-draft organizing
Tax resistance — see also Joan Baez biography 1964 onward — Publicly refused to pay the 60% of income tax she calculated went to military spending
Stop the Draft Week Oct 16, 1967 Oakland Armed Forces Induction Center, California Arrested for blocking the entrance to the induction center; jailed for 10 days at Santa Rita Rehabilitation Center
Second arrest at Oakland Induction Center — referenced in Joan Baez biography Jan 1968 Oakland, California Arrested again for blocking the induction center; served a 45-day jail sentence
Spring Mobilization to End the War Apr 15, 1967 New York City / San Francisco Participated in one of the largest demonstrations to that point (100,000–400,000 in NYC)
Woodstock Festival Aug 15–18, 1969 Bethel, New York Performed while pregnant; dedicated songs to her husband David Harris, in federal prison for draft resistance
Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam Oct 15, 1969 Multiple U.S. cities Participated in moratorium events
Christmas Bombing / Visit to Hanoi (Operation Linebacker II) — see also Joan Baez biography Dec 1972 Hanoi, North Vietnam Traveled to North Vietnam with a peace delegation; was present during the eleven-day Christmas bombing campaign

3. Pete Seeger — "Waist Deep in the Big Muddy" (1967)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Involvement
Spring Mobilization to End the War Apr 15, 1967 New York City Performed at the rally
Smothers Brothers Comedy Hour — censorship incident Sep 1967 (censored); Feb 25, 1968 (aired) CBS Television CBS censored "Waist Deep in the Big Muddy" in September 1967; after public outcry, Seeger was invited back and performed it in February 1968 — a landmark moment in protest music and media censorship
Vietnam Moratorium March on Washington Nov 15, 1969 Washington, D.C. Led approximately 500,000 demonstrators in singing "Give Peace a Chance" from the steps of the Washington Monument — one of the most iconic moments of the entire anti-war movement
Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam Oct 15, 1969 Multiple U.S. cities Participated in moratorium events

4. Phil Ochs — "Draft Dodger Rag" (1966)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Involvement
Spring Mobilization to End the War in Vietnam Apr 15, 1967 New York City Performed at the rally
March on the Pentagon Oct 21, 1967 The Pentagon, Arlington, Virginia Performed at the rally/march of ~100,000; one of the key musical performers of the day
1968 Democratic National Convention protests — see also Chicago Seven Aug 25–29, 1968 Chicago, Illinois Performed in Grant Park and Lincoln Park alongside the Yippies; Ochs was beaten by police during the violent crackdowns
Yippie "Festival of Life" — covered within DNC protests article Aug 1968 Lincoln Park, Chicago Performed as part of the counter-programming to the DNC; police tear-gassed and attacked attendees
Chile solidarity concert — referenced in Phil Ochs biography May 9, 1974 Felt Forum (MSG), New York City Organized "An Evening with Salvador Allende" in solidarity with the overthrown Chilean government; tied to the broader anti-war, anti-imperialist movement
"War Is Over" Rally — referenced in Phil Ochs biography May 11, 1975 Central Park, New York City Organized this rally to celebrate the end of the Vietnam War; ~100,000 attended; performers included Joan Baez, Harry Belafonte, Paul Simon
Note: Phil Ochs was perhaps the most protest-active artist on this entire list. For more on his life and activism, see Phil Ochs on Wikipedia.

5. Country Joe McDonald & The Fish — "I-Feel-Like-I'm-Fixin'-to-Die Rag" (1967)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Involvement
Woodstock Festival Aug 15–18, 1969 Bethel, New York Country Joe's solo performance of the "Fish Cheer" and "I-Feel-Like-I'm-Fixin'-to-Die Rag" became one of the most iconic moments of Woodstock; featured prominently in the Woodstock documentary film (1970)
Moratorium to End the War in Vietnam Oct 15, 1969 Multiple U.S. cities Participated in moratorium events

6. Crosby, Stills, Nash & Young — "Ohio" (1970)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
Kent State shootings — see also Ohio (song) May 4, 1970 Kent State University, Ohio Neil Young wrote "Ohio" within days of seeing photographs of the shooting; the single was recorded and rush-released by Atlantic Records within weeks
Woodstock Festival Aug 15–18, 1969 Bethel, New York CSNY performed (their second public performance as a group); the festival's anti-war atmosphere was central
Big Sur Folk Festival 1969 Big Sur, California Performed; the festival had strong anti-war undertones

7. Buffalo Springfield (Stephen Stills) — "For What It's Worth" (1966)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
Sunset Strip curfew riots Nov 12, 1966 and surrounding days Sunset Strip, West Hollywood, California Stephen Stills witnessed the clashes between young people and LAPD; wrote "For What It's Worth" as a direct response; the song was universally adopted by the anti-war movement

8. Jimi Hendrix — "All Along the Watchtower" (1968)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Involvement
Woodstock Festival Aug 18, 1969 (Monday morning) Bethel, New York Hendrix's performance of "The Star-Spangled Banner" — with distorted, feedback-laden evocations of bombs, sirens, and gunfire — became one of the most iconic anti-war artistic statements in American history
Note: Hendrix did not participate in organized protests. His anti-war association comes from the Woodstock performance and from "Machine Gun" (Band of Gypsys, 1970), which he explicitly introduced as being about soldiers in Vietnam. See Jimi Hendrix on Wikipedia.

9. Marvin Gaye — "What's Going On" (1971)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
No documented participation in specific organized protests — — Gaye's protest was entirely through his art; What's Going On was inspired by his brother Frankie's Vietnam service and by witnessing police brutality at anti-war protests

10. The Doors (Jim Morrison) — "The Unknown Soldier" (1968)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
No documented participation in specific organized protests — — Protest was through their music; "The Unknown Soldier" included mock-execution performance art on stage. See The Doors on Wikipedia.

11. Edwin Starr — "War" (1970)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
No documented participation in specific organized protests — — "War" (written by Norman Whitfield and Barrett Strong) reached #1 and was widely played at anti-war rallies from 1970 onward. See Edwin Starr on Wikipedia.

12. Nina Simone — "Backlash Blues" (1967)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Involvement
Selma to Montgomery marches Mar 1965 Selma → Montgomery, Alabama Performed at the final rally in Montgomery; primarily civil rights but with significant overlap with the emerging anti-war movement
Various civil rights rallies — see Nina Simone biography 1963–1968 Multiple U.S. cities Core artistic voice of the civil rights movement; wrote "Mississippi Goddam" (1964) in response to the 16th Street Baptist Church bombing and the murder of Medgar Evers
Note: Simone's primary protest identity was in the civil rights and Black Power movements rather than the anti-war movement specifically, but the two were deeply intertwined in the late 1960s. See Nina Simone on Wikipedia.

13. Barry McGuire — "Eve of Destruction" (1965)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
No well-documented participation in specific organized protests — — "Eve of Destruction" (written by P.F. Sloan) hit #1 in September 1965; one of the earliest mainstream anti-war hits; banned by many stations. See Barry McGuire on Wikipedia.

14. Freda Payne — "Bring the Boys Home" (1971)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
No well-documented participation in specific organized protests — — "Bring the Boys Home" was banned by Armed Forces Vietnam Network radio. See Freda Payne on Wikipedia.

15. Jimmy Cliff — "Vietnam" (1970)

Protest / EventDateLocationNature of Association
No well-documented participation in specific U.S. or international Vietnam protests — — "Vietnam" was a powerful reggae anti-war song reflecting international opposition, particularly from the Caribbean and developing world. See Jimmy Cliff on Wikipedia.
